Observational Study Designs

Cohort, Case–Control, and Other Designs

Philip White

Learning goals

By the end of this lecture, you should be able to:

  • Distinguish cohort and case–control study designs
  • Identify strengths and weaknesses of major observational designs
  • Understand nested, case–crossover, ecologic, and cross-sectional studies
  • Recognize common biases associated with each design
  • Choose an appropriate design for a given scientific question

Big picture: observational studies

  • Used when randomization is impossible, unethical, or impractical
  • Central goal: identify associations between exposures and outcomes
  • Causal interpretation requires care

Design choices determine what can—and cannot—be concluded.

Cohort studies: basic idea

  • Begin with exposed and nonexposed groups
  • Follow both groups forward in time
  • Compare incidence of disease (or death)

Key defining feature:

Exposure is defined before outcome occurs.

Cohort studies: what questions do they answer?

Cohort studies are well suited for questions like:

  • Does exposure increase the risk of disease?
  • How large is the absolute risk difference?
  • What is the temporal sequence of events?

They naturally produce: - Incidence rates - Risk ratios - Risk differences

Cohort study structure (2×2 table)

Disease No disease Total
Exposed a b a+b
Not exposed c d c+d

Incidence (exposed): \(I_E = \frac{a}{a+b}\)

Incidence (unexposed): \(I_U = \frac{c}{c+d}\)

Measures from cohort studies

From a cohort study we can estimate:

  • Risk ratio (relative risk)

\[RR = \frac{I_E}{I_U}\]

  • Risk difference

\[RD = I_E - I_U\]

  • Incidence rate ratios (with person-time)

These quantities have direct causal interpretations if assumptions hold.

Strengths of cohort studies

  • Directly estimate incidence
  • Establish temporality (exposure precedes outcome)
  • Can study multiple outcomes from one exposure
  • Often easier to interpret than odds ratios

Weaknesses of cohort studies

  • Often expensive and time-consuming
  • Inefficient for rare diseases
  • Several sources of bias.

Class Activity: The “HRT” Mystery

The Scenario: Does Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT) prevent heart attacks?

The Study (Nurses’ Health Study):

  • Population: 121,700 female registered nurses.
  • Exposure: Self-reported use of HRT (Current vs. Never).
  • Follow-up: 10 years of questionnaires.
  • Outcome: Coronary Heart Disease (CHD).

Step 1: Calculate the “Discovery”

Using data simplified from the 1991 Stampfer study (the data are a little more complicated):

CHD (Yes) CHD (No) Total
Current HRT 100 29,900 30,000
Never HRT 300 49,700 50,000

Task (2 Minutes):

  1. Calculate the Incidence (\(I_E\) and \(I_U\))
  2. Calculate the Risk Ratio (\(RR\))

Step 2: Discussion - Selection Bias

The “Healthy User” Effect

Women who chose to take HRT in the 80s were generally higher income, exercised more, and smoked less than those who didn’t.

Discussion Queue (3 minutes)

  • Does the \(RR\) you calculated represent the drug’s effect or the nurses’ lifestyle?
  • If we ignored these “hidden” factors, would our results be an overestimate or underestimate of the drug’s benefit?

Step 3: Discussion - Attrition Bias

Loss to Follow-up

Imagine if women who started feeling “heart flutters” (early CHD symptoms) became too fatigued to fill out their 20-page biennial surveys.

  • Discussion Queue (2 Minutes):*
  • If sick women drop out of the exposed group more often, how does that change the numerator (\(a\)) in our \(2 \times 2\) table?
  • What happens to the final \(RR\) calculation?

The Big Reveal: Cohort vs. RCT

In 2002, the Women’s Health Initiative (WHI) randomized women to HRT or Placebo.

Study Type Finding on HRT & Heart Disease
Cohort (NHS) Protective (\(RR \approx 0.56\))
RCT (WHI Trial) Harmful (\(RR \approx 1.29\))

Key Lesson: The Cohort study was biased by “Healthy Users.” It measured wealth and health awareness, not just a pill.

Selection bias (Who gets into the study?)

  • Healthy Worker Effect (Occupational Studies)
    • Workers (exposed group) are generally healthier than the general population, skewing results if compared to the public, as seen in occupational studies.
  • Healthy User/Participant Effect (Lifestyle/Diet Studies)
    • People interested in healthy lifestyles are more likely to join, leading to lower mortality and healthier behaviors in the study group than in the real world.
  • Loss to Follow-Up (Attrition Bias)
    • If sicker people (who might have the outcome) drop out more often from the exposed group than the unexposed, it makes the exposure look safer.
    • Example: In a study on hypertension and kidney disease, people without kidney issues might quit the study, leaving more sick people in the hypertensive group, inflating risk.

Information Bias (How data is collected/measured)

  • Differential Misclassification: Exposure status affects how outcomes are measured.
    • Example: Women on oral contraceptives (exposed) get more breast cancer screenings, leading to earlier detection than in non-users, creating a false link.
  • Interviewer/Observer Bias: Interviewers, knowing exposure status, might probe more or interpret subjective answers differently.

Other sources of bias

  • Confounding due to lack of randomization
    • Self-selection can lead to substantial differences between the exposed and not exposed groups.
  • Lead-Time Bias: Early detection makes survival look longer, even if the actual disease progression isn’t changed.
  • Publication Bias: Studies with statistically significant findings are more likely to get published than those without statistically significant results. This can skew the overall evidence.

Prospective vs retrospective cohorts

Prospective cohort: - Identify population now - Measure exposure going forward - Follow for outcomes

Retrospective cohort: - Use historical records - Exposure and outcome already occurred

Key point:

The logic is identical; only calendar time differs.

Case–control studies: basic idea

  • Begin with cases (people with disease) and find controls (people without disease).
  • Look backward to assess past exposure

Key feature:

Selection is based on outcome status.

Case–control study layout

Exposed Not exposed
Cases a c
Controls b d

Compare: \[\frac{a}{a+c} \quad \text{vs} \quad \frac{b}{b+d}\]

Strengths of case–control studies

  • Efficient for rare diseases
  • Faster and cheaper than cohorts
  • Can examine multiple exposures

Limitations of case–control studies

  • Cannot estimate incidence directly
  • Sensitive to similar selection bias we saw with cohort designs
  • Recall bias may distort exposure histories

Class Activity: The “Passive Surveillance” Trap

The Scenario: Researchers want to see if the HPV4 vaccine is associated with autoimmune diseases (e.g., Lupus, Rheumatoid Arthritis, POTS).

The Study (Geier and Geier 2017):

  • Database: VAERS (Vaccine Adverse Event Reporting System).
  • Design: Case-Control.
  • Cases: Female reports in VAERS with an autoimmune diagnosis.
  • Controls: Female reports in VAERS without an autoimmune diagnosis.
  • Exposure: Receipt of the HPV4 vaccine. The Result: The study found highly significant Odds Ratios for various auto-immune diseases (e.g., Lupus \(OR = 7.6\)).

Step 1: Selection Bias & “The At-Risk” Population

The Setup: The study included girls aged 6–39. However, the HPV4 vaccine is not approved for children under 9.

🗣️ Discussion Queue (4 Minutes):

  • One Issue: Autoimmune diseases are much rarer in 6-year-olds than in 20-year-olds.
  • Since 6-8 year olds cannot be vaccinated, which group (Cases or Controls) are they end up in?
  • Can this age group be exposed?
  • The Bias: If your “unvaccinated” control group is full of very young children who naturally have zero risk of the disease, how does that “tilt” the Odds Ratio?

Step 2: Discussion - What makes a “Control”?

The Setup: VAERS is a passive database; it only contains people who had an adverse event and chose to report it. Moreover, it does not require clinical verification of the event.

  • It does not represent the general healthy population.

🗣️ Discussion Queue (4 Minutes):

  • A fundamental rule: Controls must represent the population that would have become cases if they developed the disease.
  • If the controls are people reporting other injuries (like a sore arm from a flu shot), are they truly comparable to a patient with a complex autoimmune disease?
  • Methodology vs. Findings: Experts criticize this study because VAERS is meant for “Hypothesis Generation,” not “Causal Testing.” Why can’t we calculate a true denominator here?

Methodology Matters

When high-quality studies used Active Surveillance (tracking everyone, not just those who report) and Matched Controls (comparing 15-year-olds to 15-year-olds), the results changed:

Study Type Database Type Result (Autoimmune Risk)
Geier and Geier 2017 Passive (VAERS) Significant Link
Arnheim-Dahlstrom Active (Hospital Records) No Association

Why the difference?

  1. Age-Matching: Larger studies adjusted for age, education, and socioeconomic status.
  2. Medical Confirmation: Using hospital records ensures the “outcome” is a real diagnosis, not just a self-reported symptom.

Nested designs (within cohorts)

  • Combine strengths of cohort and case–control designs
  • Begin with a defined cohort
  • Perform a case–control study within it

Two main types:

  • Nested case–control
  • Case–cohort

Nested case–control studies

  • Controls selected from those at risk at the time each case occurs
  • Controls matched on calendar time and follow-up

Advantages:

  • Reduced cost
  • Minimizes recall bias
  • Higher comparability

Case–cohort studies

  • Controls selected as a random subcohort at baseline
  • Same controls can be reused for multiple outcomes

Key difference:

Controls are not time-matched to cases.

Case–crossover design

  • Each subject serves as their own control
  • Compare exposure during a short hazard period vs a control period

Used for:

  • Acute outcomes
  • Transient exposures

Case–crossover example

  • Myocardial infarction
  • Air pollution spikes

Question:

Was exposure higher immediately before the event than during a control time?

Strengths and limits of case–crossover

Strengths:

  • Controls for time-invariant confounders
  • Efficient for short-term effects

Limitations:

  • Sensitive to time trends
  • Recall bias may still occur

Ecologic studies

  • Unit of analysis = groups, not individuals

  • Compare average exposure and outcome across regions

  • Example:

    • Country-level fat intake vs breast cancer incidence

Ecologic fallacy

Associations at the group level may not hold at the individual level.

Key limitation:

  • Individual exposure–outcome links are unknown.
  • Group vs. Individual
    • The fallacy confuses characteristics of a group (average income, literacy rates, crime rates) with characteristics of individuals within that group.
  • Levels of Analysis: Data collected at a macro level (states, countries) is incorrectly applied to a micro level (people).
    • Example: If states with more immigrants have higher literacy rates, it’s an ecological fallacy to assume immigrants are more literate; the overall state literacy might be high due to the native-born population, even if immigrants within that state have lower literacy.
  • Simpson’s Paradox: A related concept where a trend seen in different groups disappears or reverses when the groups are combined, highlighting how aggregate data can be misleading.

When ecologic studies are useful

  • Hypothesis generation

  • Studying contextual effects (e.g., neighborhoods)

  • Policy-level questions

  • But they cannot establish causality alone.

Cross-sectional studies

  • Exposure and outcome measured simultaneously
  • A snapshot of a population

Also called:

  • Prevalence studies

Cross-sectional layout

Disease No Disease
Exposed a b
Not exposed c d

Can compare: - Disease prevalence by exposure - Exposure prevalence by disease

Limitations of cross-sectional studies

  • Cannot establish temporality
  • Uses prevalent, not incident, cases
  • May reflect survival rather than risk

Choosing a design

Ask:

  • Is the outcome rare?
  • Is exposure rare?
  • Is timing critical?
  • Are data already available?
  • Good design follows the scientific question.

Which design would you choose to study:

  • Long-term effects of air pollution?
  • A rare cancer?
  • Acute triggers of heart attacks?

Summary: Choosing Your Study Design

Design Starting Point Direction of Inquiry Best For… Major “Red Flag”
Cohort Exposure Forward to Outcome Rare exposures; Incidence Selection Bias (Healthy User Effect)
Case-Control Outcome Backward to Exposure Rare diseases; Fast/Cheap Selection Bias (Control selection)
Case-Crossover Outcome “Hazard” vs “Control” time Acute triggers (triggers) Information Bias (Recall)
Cross-Sectional Snapshot Simultaneous Prevalence; Surveying Temporality (Chicken vs Egg)
Ecologic Populations Group Comparisons Policy; Environment Ecologic Fallacy

Wrap-up

  • Observational designs trade control for feasibility

  • Each design answers different questions

  • Understanding limitations is essential for causal reasoning
